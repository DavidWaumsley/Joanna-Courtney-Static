Women of the Ancient World

For many years I wanted to write about the women of the early church – women who were comprehensively written out of the story of Christianity. This is often the case in history, but rarely with such cruel injustice. If you read between and, indeed behind, the lines of the very carefully curated bible books, you find a vast range of other sources that were simply not included in the ‘authorised’ version when it was issued – by men.

Even as they stand, the so-called ‘gospels’ are inconclusive and contradictory, and the various letters are undoubtedly a tiny proportion of a wide-ranging set of correspondence amongst a community feeling its way (rather than a set of dyed-in-the-wool instructions as they are often considered).

I wanted to know more of the real story. What history revealed was a rich community of people that not only included women, but was, in many cases, driven by them. Most of the early Christian churches were ‘house churches’, often run by housewives, widows and businesswomen, and definitely sustained by them. Jesus spoke to minorities – to lepers and to the poor and to women. Early Christianity was a wonderfully loving and inclusive religion, as he intended, and Mary Magdala was at the heart of that. In neither my novels, Salome, or Mary of Magdala, do I seek to redefine Christianity, but in both, I seek to tell the truth about its genesis.

My interest in Cleopatra was sparked when someone pointed out to me that her reign was closer in time to the invention of the iphone than the building of the Pyramids of Gisa. This is a woman who was ruling on the edge of the modern world, alongside Julius Caesar – her lover – and King Herod the Great. That was just thirty years before Christ trod the earth, so I felt her story was an interesting lead into those of Salome and Mary.

Exciting times were afoot around the new millenium and the life of these three legendary, if much misunderstood women, were at the heart of huge change. I hope readers enjoy them.

Shakespeare’s Queens

Shakespeare was an arch-dramatist - a man with a wonderful eye for the best way to take a tale and make it grip an audience and hold them in his spell all the way to the climactic end. It is why he has endured for so long. What Shakespeare was not, however, was a man who would let truth get in the way of a good story. Take Lady Macbeth, for example, to Shakespeare a vicious, murderous usurper on a high-speed path to self-destruction but in real life, an anointed Queen of Scotland for some fifteen prosperous years from 1040. As soon as I discovered this contradiction I wanted to know more about her and the other heroines Shakespeare might not have represented as faithfully as he could have done- such as Ofelia, warrior and staunch protector of Prince Hamlet in his fight to reclaim the Danish throne from his uncle in 600 AD, and Cordelia, daughter of Chieftain Leir and one of the valued leaders of the Coritani tribe in 500 BC who had to fight family treachery to keep her lands and her people. These, I felt were women the world should get to know, not just through Shakespeare's versions of their lives but in all their true glory. I hope you enjoy doing so.

Queens of Conquest

1066 was the most momentous year in England's history, with action from the moment King Edward died on January 6th to the eventual coronation of King William on Christmas day. In between battles raged in all parts of the country, from York to Hastings, sucking most of the ppulation into the fight for the throne. Even now many people are familiar with the three key contenders - Harold of Wessex, Harald Hardrada and William the Conqueror. But what of their wives? What of Edith of Mercia, Elizaveta of Kiev and Mathilda of Flanders? These were not just little women, backing up their husbands from the bedroom, but powerful rulers with a strong understanding of the mechanics of power and the influence to help change the events of 1066. For too long their stories have been neglected and The Queens of the Conquest novels look to redress that. I hope you enjoy them.
